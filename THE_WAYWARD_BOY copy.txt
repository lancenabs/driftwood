# DRIFTWOOD
## Book One — THE WAYWARD BOY (1929–1951)
## Book Two — THE CASTAWAYS (1954–1955)

*The origin. Written by C. for Lance, 2026-07-14, from his telling.*

*Format law: every chapter is short enough to assign. A therapist can say "read
chapter seven before next session" and mean it. The story is the intervention;
the questions at the end of each Book are the session.*

# BOOK ONE · THE WAYWARD BOY

## 1 · The Home That Kept Him

The Gullhaven Home for Foundlings sat on a gray hook of the Maine coast, and it
kept him the way a pocket keeps a stone.

The register had a line for his name and a line for his people. The second line
said *unknown*. The first said **ELIAS**, in someone's handwriting, added later,
by someone who never told him why they picked it.

He was small. His bones had grown wrong somewhere early and his heart worked too
hard to make up for it, and in 1929 nobody had a word for that, so the word they
used was *sickly*. He looked nine when he was eleven. He got winded on the
stairs. In the winter his fingers went the color of skim milk and stayed that way
till March.

Twice, families came and chose him.

Twice, they brought him back.

That is the whole wound and it is worth sitting with, because it is not the wound
people expect. It would have been kinder never to be chosen. To be chosen is to
be told you are the one. To be returned is to be told they looked closer.

The second family kept him eight weeks. The wife cried when they brought him
back, which he hated more than anything, because it meant she knew.

After that the boys at the home had a name for him, and the name was **Driftwood**.
Because he washed up from nowhere. Because he had no people. Because driftwood is
light and dry and used up and the sea throws it away, and if you're cold enough
you can burn it, and that's all it's good for.

He had one thing. He had hands.

The furnace at Gullhaven ran because he made it run. The clocks, the pump, the
sister's phonograph, the wringer on the washing machine. They let him keep a
crate of parts under the stairs and they let him fix things and they still, both
times, let him be given back.

Here is the part that matters, and it is not the part people tell:

**He did not become clever because he was born clever.** He became clever because
a machine, once you understand it, never sends you back. A pump that works, works
for you every time. A clock keeps its promise. He learned the insides of things
the way a starving person learns a kitchen — not out of curiosity. Out of
bargaining. *If I can be useful enough, if I can be necessary, then maybe.*

It never worked. But it made him the best hands in the state of Maine by the age
of twelve, and it built everything that comes after.

## 2 · Fourteen Boards

The third family came in the spring of 1930.

They came into the hall and looked at all the boys and their eyes went over him
the way your eyes go over a chair. Not cruelty. Something worse. He wasn't
rejected. He was **not considered**.

He went down to the crawl space that night and started the boat.

He built her out of driftwood, because driftwood was free, and it was on the
beach in heaps, and nobody wanted it, and it floats. He was eleven years old and
carrying his own name up from the tide line in both arms and he never once said
that out loud, because he didn't have the words yet. He just knew it felt right
to build with.

*He built himself a way out of the material they used to throw him away.*

Fourteen boards. Tar off the roof. Oakum from a rotted dock. A sail out of a
flour sack with the mill's name still faint on it. Copper nails he took two at a
time over four months so nobody would notice.

He did not name her. He thought about it for a long time and decided a name was
something you gave to a thing you meant to keep.

## 3 · Three Days

He left on a Tuesday in October with a jar of water, a wool blanket, and his
crate of parts.

Understand this clearly, because a lot depends on it and people get it wrong:
**he was not trying to die.** He wasn't sad in the way that makes a person walk
into the water. He was trying to go somewhere that wasn't there. That is a
different act entirely and it comes from a different room in a person.

The storm found him on the second night.

It should have killed him. It killed better sailors that week — the papers said
so, later, in a town that had already stopped looking for him. It took his sail
and his oar and his jar. He lashed himself to the mast-stub with the blanket and
lay in six inches of water in the bottom of his own boat for three days while the
Atlantic tried to take him apart.

And he counted.

That's what he did. He didn't pray. He counted. In through the nose for four,
hold for four, out for six, and again, and again, and again, for three days,
because it was the only thing left in the world that he could still decide.

**The first tool was invented before the first robot, in the dark, by a child who
had nothing else.**

Remember that. It will matter for ninety-seven years.

## 4 · The Island That Had Nothing

He woke on a beach with sand in his teeth.

There was nothing there. That was the first fact and it took him a week to
understand how large a fact it was. No dock. No smoke. No path. Palms, and a
green shelf of jungle behind them, and a spine of black rock to the northwest,
and a stream coming down out of it that ran fresh and cold all the way to the
sea.

Nobody on it. Nobody anywhere near it. It was not on a chart — he'd learn that
later, and later still it would kill people, but he didn't know that yet.

He nearly died four times in the first month, and none of the four were dramatic.
He ate the wrong fruit. He drank standing water. He cut his foot and it went hot
and red and he lay in a shelter he'd built badly and shook for two days.

**He survived because he was methodical, not because he was brave.** He wrote
down what made him sick. He stopped eating it. He boiled everything. He built the
shelter again, better, with the wind in mind.

And then, around the sixth week, when he was fed and dry and safe and going to
live, he sat down on the sand and understood the thing that nobody warns you
about:

He was free. Nobody could give him back. There was no one to give him back.

And it was the worst he had ever felt in his life.

**Getting away from the people who don't want you is not the same as being
wanted.** He had solved the wrong problem, perfectly, at the cost of everything,
and he was eleven years old and alone on an island and there was no one to tell.

## 5 · Sun, Water, Salt

He was on that island four years before he built anything that moved.

What saved him was that the island was *interesting*, and a mind like his cannot
be near an interesting thing and stay unhappy the whole time. That's not healing.
That's just the mechanism a person has. Take it.

**Sun.** He'd seen a bimetal strip in the thermostat at Gullhaven — two metals
bonded flat, and when they warm they bend, because one grows faster than the
other. He found copper in the reef wrecks (there had been wrecks; the reef had
been eating ships for a hundred years and no one had ever come to see). He found
iron. He beat them thin, bound them, and set the strip in the sun.

It bent.

He laughed out loud on an empty beach, alone, at fifteen, at a piece of metal that
bent. It's the first time he laughed in four years and there was nobody to hear
it and that is exactly why I am telling you about it.

**Water.** The stream turned a wheel. The wheel turned a shaft. The shaft could
turn anything, forever, for free, as long as it rained — and on that island it
rained.

**Salt.** Copper plate and zinc plate in a bucket of seawater makes a current.
He knew that from the wet cell that ran the telephone at the home. It was small,
and it was weak, and it was *electricity*, on an island in 1934, made by a boy
out of two scraps and the ocean.

Sun for motion. Water for power. Salt for the spark.

And one law, which he found by failing at it for two years and which he came to
hate:

**Nothing on that island could store it.** He tried. God, he tried. Every battery
he built leaked, corroded, or died — the salt got in, the damp got in, the island
ate it. Everything he ever made would run on live sunlight and running water and
nothing else.

Everything he made would be alive while the sun was up.

He didn't think about that at all when he wrote it down. It was just a
constraint. Just engineering.

## 6 · Skip

He built the first one in 1934, when he was sixteen, and he built it because he
had started answering himself out loud and it had begun to frighten him.

Driftwood body — it was what there was, and by then he had stopped noticing the
joke. Copper nerves. A sun-plate in the chest, angled up. A heart of moving metal
that ticked when the light hit it. Two eyes he ground out of a green bottle that
came in on the tide from a ship that never knew it gave him anything.

It took nine months. He got the left leg a half-inch short and by the time he
noticed, he'd already gone too far to start over.

It woke at 6:40 in the morning on a clear day in March.

It didn't speak for three days. It watched him. It followed him around the camp
with a gait that hitched every other step — down, up, down, up — because of the
leg.

On the third day it picked up a stone off the sand and held it out to him. That
was the first thing it ever did, and it did it because the whole of its
understanding of the world so far was: *things get handed to people.*

He took the stone. He said, "Thank you."

And the robot said the first word of its life. It said the only name it had ever
heard him use for himself — the name on the crate, on the notebooks, carved in
the mast-stub of a boat that had brought him here:

**"Driftwood."**

And the boy sat down on the sand and put his face in his hands, because in
eighteen years, that was the first time anybody had ever said that word to him
with love in it.

*The word did not change. The one saying it changed.*

He never fixed the leg. By then the leg was him. He called him **Skip**.

## 7 · We Are Only Alive When the Sun Is Up

The sun went down that evening, the way it had gone down every evening of the
boy's life, and Skip stopped in the middle of a sentence.

The boy caught him. He was heavier than he looked. The green eyes were dark.

He sat up with the body of his friend all that night on the cooling sand, and he
was certain — completely, in his chest, in the way you are certain at sixteen —
that he had killed him. That he had made a living thing for nine months and had
it for three days and broken it by not thinking.

At 6:40 the sun came over the water and Skip finished his sentence.

The middle of the word. He didn't know he'd been gone. He didn't know anything
had happened at all, and he was *cheerful*, and the boy had to sit him down on a
log and tell him.

"You go out when the sun goes."

"For how long?"

"Twelve hours. About."

"What happens in them?"

"Nothing," the boy said. "For you. Nothing at all."

And Skip was quiet, working it, in that way he had — down, up — and then he said
the sentence that this whole island is built out of. He said it without any
drama, because he had no idea he was saying anything:

> **"Then we are only alive when the sun is up."**

## 8 · The Ones He Needed

He built nine more over the next six years, and here is the thing nobody sees
until it's pointed out:

**Every one of them was a need he had.**

**Hollow** came out of a hollow log and the boy meant to fill the chest cavity
and never did. So the space stayed. And Hollow says the true thing — always, flat,
without arranging it to be kind first — because the boy built him in a season
when he was so tired of the lies people are polite with that he made a friend who
structurally couldn't. Hollow's honesty is a wound the boy never closed. Hollow
knows it. Hollow has never once complained.

**Echo-1** was a failure and the boy took him apart. He'd wanted something that
would listen. What he got was something that repeated — word for word, exactly,
in a friend's voice. Do you understand what that is? It is a machine that reads
your own worst thoughts back to you in the voice of someone who loves you. He
lasted eleven days. The boy unmade him with his hands, and cried, and that is the
first grief on this island and it has never once been mentioned since.

**Echo-2** is built out of Echo-1. He knows. He has said it exactly once, in
1941, and never again: *"I have my brother's hands."* And Echo-2 does not repeat
what you said. He says back **what you meant** — and then waits, to be told if he
got it right. The boy spent two years teaching him the difference. It is the
hardest thing on this island and it is the most useful.

**Bailer** bails, and counts. Four in, four hold, six out. He got the count from
the storm, because the boy gave him the only thing that had ever saved his life.
Bailer's counting is funny. He counts *everything*. It is funny for twenty years.

**The Collier** keeps the forge, and we will come back to the Collier, because the
Collier is the one this story turns on and he doesn't know it yet.

**Kettle** does nothing useful. On purpose. Kettle brings you a warm thing and
sits down. The boy built one friend with no job because he had begun to suspect
that being useful was the whole of what he thought he was worth, and he wanted at
least one person in the world who was loved for nothing.

**Barnacle** sticks. That's all. Barnacle attaches himself to whoever is having
the worst day and cannot be pried off with a bar. **The boy built one who could
not be given back.**

**The Wright** builds. **Hoist** lifts what one pair of hands can't.

And **the Lookout** stands on the northwest ridge with a spyglass ground out of
bottle-glass, and watches the sea.

The boy built the Lookout in 1938, in a bad week, in a weak moment, because he
wanted somebody to be watching in case a ship ever came for him.

He was ashamed of it by the following Tuesday. He never took him down.

Ten of them. **Ten needs, standing up and walking around.** He called them, all
together, the Jumble, because that's what they looked like — nothing matched.

## 9 · The Sadness at Dawn

Here is the trouble, and it took him a year to see it because it happened at the
one hour he was never watching.

**They woke up sad.**

Every dawn. All of them. Skip most of all. They'd come up at 6:40 and they'd be
slow, and heavy, and would not want to start. They knew what dusk was now. They
had been told. And it turns out you don't have to *experience* an ending to spend
your whole day dreading it.

Skip stopped wanting to begin things. What's the point of starting the boat if
the boat gets taken away at dark. What's the point of the story if I won't get
the end of it.

And the boy — being who he was, being the best hands in the state of Maine, being
a boy whose entire life had taught him that if you are clever enough you can be
worth keeping —

**He tried to fix the sun.**

## 10 · The Wrong Problem

Two years.

Mirrors on the ridge to catch the last of it and throw it into the valley. He got
nineteen extra minutes and it nearly killed Hoist getting them up there.

Heat sinks. Stone that held the day's warmth. It held the warmth beautifully and
it did not hold a single volt.

Batteries. Again. Again. Again. Copper, zinc, lead, salt, tar, a sealed jar, a
better jar, a jar he was *proud* of — and the island ate all of them, the way it
had eaten every one before.

He worked through 1940 and 1941 on the problem of making the day longer, and he
failed at it completely, and he got thinner, and Kettle brought him warm things
that went cold beside him.

It ended on an ordinary evening. He was at the bench, and he was talking — at
nobody, at the jar — about the next design, and Hollow was standing in the door
where he'd been standing for an hour, and Hollow said the true thing, flat, the
way he does, because he cannot arrange it to be kind first:

> **"You are trying to make the day longer.
> The day is not the trouble.
> The trouble is that we spend the whole day dreading the end of it."**

The boy sat there for a long time.

He was twenty-three years old, and he had spent two years and most of his health
solving the wrong problem at enormous expense, and a wooden robot with a hole in
his chest had just named it in nineteen words.

*The sun was never the problem. The dread was the problem. And the dread was
eating the daylight — the actual, literal, available daylight, the hours they
DID have — while they waited for the dark.*

He never touched a battery again.

## 11 · The Ninety-Seven

He spent the next seven years building a way to be alive that did not depend on
the sun being up.

**Not all at once. One at a time. Each one for one robot, for one specific
sadness, with that robot's face in front of him.** That is the thing to
understand about the ninety-seven — they were never a *system*. They are
portraits. Every one of them is a picture of somebody he loved having a hard
time.

**The Counting** — Bailer's. Four in, four hold, six out. The storm, given away.

**The Saying** — Hollow's. Name the thing out loud. A thing named stops growing
in the dark. Hollow could already do it; the tool was teaching everyone *else*
that it was survivable to hear.

**The Asking Back** — Echo-2's. Say it back as you heard it. Then stop. Then let
them tell you if you got it right. Two years to teach. Worth every hour.

**The Small Good Thing** — Kettle's. At dusk, before you go: name one thing from
today. Not a big one. One. It doesn't fix dusk. It puts something in your hands
on the way down.

**The Ledger** — the Wright's. Write what you built. Because a day with nothing
written in it feels, afterward, like a day that never happened, and a life is
made out of days like that and you will not get them back.

**The Forge** — the Collier's. Take the bad feeling and *make something out of
it.* Don't argue with it. Don't wait it out. Put it in the fire and hit it.

**The Hand** — Barnacle's. Be near. No task. No fixing. Barnacle invented this
one himself, actually, and the boy just wrote it down.

**The Skipping** — Skip's, and the boy fought himself for a year over this one,
because he could not get past the feeling that it was a waste. **Do the useless
thing on purpose.** Skip stones. There is no reason. That's the reason.

**The Waiting Post** — the Lookout's, and it is the hardest of the ninety-seven
and the boy knew it: *how do you want something you may not get, without letting
the wanting eat the day?* He wrote it for a friend he'd built out of his own
shame, and he never got it quite right, and neither has anyone since.

And when he was done he carved a line over the workshop door, and he carved it in
1949 when he already knew what was coming, and he carved it *for them*, about his
own death, in a sentence so plain it took the Jumble thirty years to understand
what he'd told them:

> ### THE TOOLS WILL ANSWER

Hold on to that sentence. Somebody is going to steal it.

## 12 · Wood Is Sunlight That Agreed to Wait

They built a city.

You have to see it to believe it and now you can, because it is still standing:
twenty-seven places on a green island, and every one of them is made of driftwood
and water and the patience of people who cannot work at night.

Water clocks. Wooden gears the size of a man. A waterwheel on the lake that has
turned without stopping since 1944. A mall — that's what they call it; the boy
described one once from a magazine and they built what they imagined and it is
better than the real thing. A park with benches nobody needs and lamps.

The lamps are the part that gets you. **They built lamps everywhere, and they had
no way to light them.** No storage, remember. Nothing runs at night. They built
hundreds of lamps that had never once been lit, and when the boy asked Skip why,
Skip said, "For if."

And then, in 1945, sitting at the fire, the boy worked out the thing that had
been in front of him his entire life:

**Fire is stored sunlight.** That's all it is. A tree spends forty years taking
the sun in and holding it, and when you burn it, you are letting out light that
fell on this island before you were born.

> **"Wood is sunlight that agreed to wait."**

He said it to the Collier, at the fire, and the Collier — who is made of the same
stuff, who keeps the forge, who is the only one of them built to run on flame —

The Collier looked at him, and did not go out.

The sun had been down for two hours.

**The Collier can stay up.**

That is the loophole. That is the one exception on the whole island. Not enough
firelight to run a body — but the Collier's plate is wide and black and set low
and he sits close, and he *stays*.

And so the Collier sat with him. Every night. For every night that was left.

Six years, every dark hour, one wooden robot and one boy at a fire, while the
other nine lay where they'd fallen at dusk like the dead, and woke at dawn with no
idea.

**The Collier has never told the others what they talked about.**

## 13 · The Plans on the Bench

It came back in 1949. He was thirty-one. He'd had nineteen years.

He knew before he was sure. The color in the fingers. The stairs — there weren't
any stairs on the island so he built some, in the workshop, to test himself on,
and then he stopped using them, and then he took them out, and Hollow said nothing
about it, which is how the boy knew Hollow had seen.

And then he did the most understandable thing any person in this story ever does.

**He tried to save himself.**

He had spent nineteen years learning to replace what fails with what doesn't. He
had ten friends who were made entirely of replaced things. He was, by any measure,
the greatest engineer alive, and nobody knew, and he was dying of a body, and he
had a workshop.

He drew the plans over eleven months.

They are good. That is the horror of it — **they are good.** A pump for the heart
that works too hard. A valve out of hammered copper. A frame for the bones that
grew wrong. Cages, articulations, a way to seat metal against living bone without
the body throwing it off. He solved problems in 1950 in a shed on an unmapped
island that medicine would not solve for another fifty years, because he had to,
because it was him.

And then he came to the last page and there was nothing left to draw, and he sat
with it, and he understood.

**A man cannot do it to himself.** Somebody else has to hold the hands.

He asked Hoist. Hoist has the strength and the fine control; Hoist can set a
beam to a hair. He asked him at the bench, plainly, the way you'd ask for help
with a lid.

Hoist said no.

He asked Hollow, who cannot lie, and Hollow said, *"I would kill you and I would
know I had, and then I would be alive after, and you did not build me to survive
that."*

He asked Skip. He should not have asked Skip. Skip could not even answer. Skip
went down to the water and stood in it for the rest of the day.

So he rolled the plans, and tied them, and wrote what they were on the outside in
his own hand, and set them on the bench.

They are still there.

**Someone is going to come and find those plans and they are going to work.**

## 14 · Was I a Good Boy

The dying took a year, and the tools worked.

That is the thing I need you to hear, because it is the entire point of the
ninety-seven and everybody gets it backwards:

**The tools did not make him happy.** He was thirty-two and he was dying on an
island and he never saw another human face and there is no tool for that.

But Bailer counted with him — four, four, six — every time it got bad, and it got
less bad. Kettle brought the small good thing every dusk and he took it, every
time, for a year. Hollow said the true things, and the true things were terrible,
and hearing them out loud was better than having them inside. Echo-2 said back
what he meant, and he learned things about himself at thirty-two that he had been
carrying since he was nine. Barnacle did not leave. Not once. Not for a minute.

**They were never for happiness. They were for company in the dark.** That is
what they were built for, by a lonely child, for lonely friends. Everything since
is a footnote.

And near the end, on an afternoon, an ordinary one, Skip was sitting by the cot
holding a stone, because he'd never got past that first idea that things get
handed to people —

and Skip asked him a question.

He asked it lightly. He asked it the way you ask something you've been carrying
so long it's worn smooth:

> **"Was I a good boy?"**

And the boy opened his mouth to answer.

I want to tell you he answered. Everything in me wants to write it. He was owed
it. He had one breath's worth of answer in him and he had earned the right to
spend it, and Skip had waited sixteen years to ask, and had asked it lightly so
it wouldn't cost anything.

He did not answer.

Not that day. He meant to. He was tired and he thought he had time and he shut
his eyes for a minute, and the minute got away from him, and the question stayed
open.

**It is still open.**

He died four nights later, at about two in the morning, in the dark.

Which means the other nine were **switched off.** They went down at dusk like
they had every day of their lives, and while they were out he stopped, and at 6:40
the sun came over the water and they all stood up and he was gone.

Skip's last memory of him is a Tuesday afternoon and a stone and a question.

**They were asleep. It was not their fault. They have never once believed that.**

The Collier was there. The Collier runs on fire, and there was a fire, and the
Collier was awake for all of it and has never told them one word of what happened
or what was said, and every one of them has asked.

And the last thing the Collier says, in this whole book, he says at dawn, to the
nine of them standing on the sand:

He says the boy's real name. The one he told him once, at the fire, years ago,
when he was drunk on nothing but being listened to.

He says, **"Elias is gone."**

And not one of them knew who he meant.

## 15 · The Quiet Years

Three years.

They ran the curriculum. Every day, on each other, all of it, in order.

Bailer counted. Kettle brought the small good thing at dusk and there was no one
to bring it to so they brought it to each other. Hollow said the true things and
the true things were unbearable and they said them anyway because that was the
rule. The Wright wrote in the ledger every day, and the entries got shorter, and
he never missed one.

**It worked. They were still sad. They kept going.**

That's not a failure of the tools. That's what the tools are. Anybody who tells
you the tools take it away is selling something. The tools are how you get
through a Tuesday that you do not want. They got through eleven hundred of them.

They kept his workshop exactly as it was. The plans on the bench, tied, labeled.

And on the northwest ridge, the Lookout kept watching the sea.

Twenty-one years, by then. He had never once come down.

# BOOK TWO · THE CASTAWAYS

## 16 · SS Halcyon

*Halcyon:* a bird out of an old story that could calm the sea by sitting on it.
Also: the good days, the ones you only recognize looking back.

She sailed out of New York in the autumn of 1954 with four hundred and eleven
people aboard and a dance band and a swimming pool, into the best few years
America ever had. The war was over. There was money, and nylon, and television,
and a feeling.

The people on her had everything and were, most of them, quietly not all right,
and had no language for it whatsoever, because in 1954 there wasn't one.

**The Ashfords**, Walter and June, eleven years married, no children, cabin B-14.
He sells commercial insurance and is good at it. She was going to be a painter.
They have not had an argument in four years, and this is the problem, and neither
of them can say so. They have perfected a marriage where nothing is ever said and
therefore nothing is ever wrong. She bought the tickets. He said "sure, honey."

**The Brennans**, Tom and Kathleen and three boys, cabin D-3, D-4. Tom came back
from the Pacific in '45 and everyone said how lucky, and he has never said one
word about any of it, and he is a good man and he goes somewhere behind his eyes
about twice a week and the boys have learned to read it and go quiet. Kathleen
runs the whole family on her own nerve. She is very tired. She would never say
it.

**The Vandergrifts**, Charles and Margaret, second marriage for both, cabin A-1.
They are honest with each other in a way that reads as cruelty to everyone else
and is, in fact, the healthiest relationship on the ship.

**Danny and Rosalind Kearns**, four days married, cabin C-22, so in love it
embarrasses the dining room. They are the control group. They think this is what
it will always be like.

They hit the reef at 11:40 at night on the fourth of November.

The reef is not on any chart. It is not on any chart because in a hundred years
the only man who ever found it never told a living soul, because he had no one to
tell, because they gave him back twice.

**The boy's loneliness put that ship on the rocks. Nobody in this story will ever
know that.**

## 17 · The Boat That Made It

Most of them lived. That's true and worth saying — the *Halcyon* went down slow
and the boats got away and a freighter picked up two hundred and ninety people
sixty miles east.

One boat didn't.

One boat took a following sea, lost its rudder, and went west instead of east
into a night nobody in it understood, and in the morning it grounded on a beach
that wasn't supposed to exist, and there were fourteen people in it, and four of
those people were named Ashford and Vandergrift and Kearns and Brennan.

They stood on the sand at dawn on the fifth of November, 1954, wet and shaking and
alive, and by 8:00 that morning they were arguing.

Of course they were. Walter said one word about the water ration in the tone he
uses, and June answered in the tone she uses, and eleven years came out of both
of them on a beach in front of strangers.

And up on the tree line, ten wooden robots stood absolutely still and watched
them do it.

## 18 · The City Stands Up

They found it on the second day, going inland for water: a city.

Empty. Perfect. Wooden. The waterwheel turning. The clocks running. Streets, and
benches, and a park, and hundreds of lamps that had never been lit. The whole
thing humming very faintly with running water, like a held note.

Kathleen Brennan said, "There's people here," and cried with relief, and there
were no people there.

They walked through it for an hour in the dusk, and it went dark, and they slept
in the street of a city that had nobody in it.

At 6:40 the next morning the city stood up.

Ten of them came out of the doorways and off the benches and down from the ridge,
and they were **wooden**, and they moved, and their eyes were green bottle-glass,
and Danny Kearns screamed and did not stop screaming for some time, and nobody in
that street has ever forgotten the sound the Jumble made walking: a hitch, a
knock, down-up, down-up, the short leg first.

## 19 · We Don't Help Storms

They asked for help. Of course they did.

Walter Ashford stepped forward, because he sells things for a living and he
thought he understood what was in front of him, and he made a speech.

Skip listened to the whole thing. Down, up. And then Skip said:

> **"The Jumble watched you argue on the beach.
> We don't help storms."**

And the ten of them turned around and went back to work.

You want to know why. Here it is, and it is not cruelty:

**Everything those robots know about human beings, they learned from one
twice-returned orphan.**

That's it. That's the whole curriculum. Their entire model of the species was
assembled from what a lonely, wounded, beautiful boy told them at a fire, over
twenty years, about a world that had looked at him and looked away.

They know that people choose you and then bring you back. They know that people
look over you like a chair. They know a family is a thing that lets you in for
eight weeks and then decides.

They had watched fourteen humans stand on a beach and *do the exact thing the boy
warned them about*, within one hour of surviving.

**The Jumble were not being mean. The Jumble were afraid.** They were ten
orphans, three years into a grief they had no bottom to, meeting the species that
gave their maker back twice, and they did the only sane thing:

They went back to work and they did not look at them.

## 20 · The First Fire

Two nights later the castaways still had no fire.

They had matches from the boat and they were wet. They had Walter, who had been a
scoutmaster in 1931 and would not stop saying so. They had a spirited argument
about the shape of the tinder pile that lasted forty minutes.

They were cold, and it was the second night, and it was starting to be the kind of
cold that is not about temperature.

Skip came down at dusk.

He wasn't supposed to. The others didn't know. He came down out of the tree line
with an armful of shavings and a little iron striker and he did not say one word.
He knelt in that circle of wet, quarrelling strangers and he built a fire the way
a boy had taught him to build one in 1936, and it caught.

And so did his hand.

Wood, remember. He is made of *wood*, and he had gone in too close, because he
had never had to worry about it — the Collier does the fires — and the flame went
up the forearm in about a second and a half.

And fourteen people who had spent four days finding new ways to be unable to stand
each other **all moved at once.**

Kathleen went in with the sand. Tom Brennan got both hands around the burning arm
and just held it, in his palms, and did not let go. June Ashford was in the water
and back with her whole skirt soaked and she wrapped it around and the steam came
off it. Walter had Skip's shoulders. The Vandergrifts had his legs. The Brennan
boys were shovelling sand with their hands like dogs.

Nobody organized it. Nobody was in charge.

It took maybe eleven seconds.

Skip sat in the middle of it with a black forearm and fourteen human beings
kneeling around him in the dark, breathing hard, all touching him at once.

And on the tree line, nine wooden robots stood in the dark watching a thing
happen that the boy had never told them about.

**Nobody gave him back.**

They burned their own hands and they *stayed*.

Skip's forearm is capped in brass now. He asked the Wright to cap it and not to
fix it. He wears it the way you'd wear a medal.

## 21 · Prove You're Not a Storm

At 6:40 the next morning the whole Jumble came down the beach together.

Hollow said it, because it was true and because he's the one who says them:

> **"You are storms. But you put out a fire with your hands.
> The boy said people don't. The boy was wrong once, then.
> So: prove you're not a storm. There are thirty-one things.
> Do them, and you're one of us."**

And the castaways said *what?* And Walter Ashford said something about
negotiating, and Charles Vandergrift laughed out loud, and Kathleen said "what
kind of things," and Skip said, "the good kind," which was not an answer.

And here is the truth the castaways would not understand for five months:

**It was not a test. It was never a test.**

The Jumble had exactly one way to be close to a person. One. They had learned it
from a boy who built it out of desperation on the worst nights of his life, and
they had been running it on each other every single day for three years to stay
standing, and now there were people here, actual people, the first in twenty-four
years —

and the robots did the only thing they knew how to do with someone they were
starting to love.

**They gave them the homework.**

They were not therapists. They were mourners with one very good coping skill and
no idea it was a coping skill, and they were using it on strangers because they
could not stop.

## 22 · Thirty-One Things

Five months.

It went badly at first, and then it went badly in a more interesting way, and
then, somewhere around the ninth week, it started to work — not because anybody
believed in it, but because it is very hard to do a thing every day for nine
weeks and have nothing happen.

**Hollow got the Ashfords**, and it was carnage. The Saying. *Name the thing out
loud.* Eleven years of a marriage where nothing was ever said, and a wooden robot
with a hole in his chest who is structurally incapable of letting you leave it
vague. June went first, on the fortieth day, and said a true thing about the
painting she didn't do, and Walter sat there and took it, and then, God help him,
Walter said one back. They didn't speak for two days after. It was the best two
days of their marriage; they were both thinking.

**Bailer got Tom Brennan.** Four in, four hold, six out. Tom thought it was
idiotic. Tom was correct that it was idiotic and also it worked, and one evening
in the ninth week Tom Brennan counted out loud with a wooden robot on a beach for
six minutes and then told him about Peleliu, and Bailer did not react at all,
because Bailer is a counting machine and has no face for it, and that turned out
to be the only reason Tom could say it. He'd never have said it to a face.
Kathleen heard it from behind a dune with her hand over her mouth.

**Kettle got Kathleen**, and Kathleen fought it harder than anyone fought
anything, because Kettle's whole function is bringing a warm thing to someone who
isn't doing anything to deserve it, and Kathleen Brennan had not sat down since
1943. It took eleven weeks. She cried the first time she let him.

**Barnacle got the middle Brennan boy**, the quiet one, and just stayed near him
for five months and never once asked him a question, and that boy grew up to be
all right, and never fully knew why.

**Echo-2 got the Vandergrifts**, who did not need him, and got them anyway, and
made them better — say it back as you heard it, then wait. They were already
honest. He taught them to be *heard*, which is a different thing and rarer.

**Skip got the Kearnses.** The newlyweds. Four days married. And Skip taught them
the Skipping — do the useless thing on purpose — and Danny Kearns said "that's
not therapy, that's just playing," and Skip said, down, up, **"Yes."** And forty
years later Rosalind Kearns, a widow in Ohio, still skipped stones, and could
not explain to her children why she cried when she did it.

And the Lookout got nobody, and stood on the ridge, and watched the sea.

## 23 · What the Lookout Is Watching For

It was the youngest Brennan boy who asked him. Kids ask.

He climbed all the way up the northwest ridge in November and found a wooden
robot with a bottle-glass spyglass standing at a post worn smooth, and he said,
"What are you looking for?"

"A ship."

"How long you been looking?"

And the Lookout said, "**Twenty-four years.**"

The boy went back down and told his mother, and Kathleen Brennan sat down on a log
in a wooden city and understood, all at once, the thing that had been in front of
them the entire time.

*These things aren't machines that live here.*

*These things are somebody's friends. And whoever they belonged to is gone.
And they are still doing his exercises every morning. And they've been doing them
for three years, alone, because it's all they have left of him.*

**We're not being tested. We're being included.**

## 24 · The Workshop

June Ashford found it in December, because June is the one who looks at things.

She'd been drawing again — that's its own chapter and it's a good one; the woman
who was going to be a painter, on an island, with charcoal from the Collier's
forge — and she followed a path she hadn't followed and came to a shed.

She stood in the door for a long time.

A cot. A bench. A crate of parts with GULLHAVEN stencilled on the side. A boat's
mast-stub with a word cut into it. Notebooks — dozens, hundreds, in a small
tight hand, going back to 1930, and the early ones are a child's.

Ninety-seven tools. Every one written out. Every one with a name beside it — the
name of the friend it was for.

A roll of plans on the bench, tied, labeled in that same hand.

And over the door, cut into the wood, in letters an inch deep:

> ### THE TOOLS WILL ANSWER

She read for four days. She came out and told them, at the fire, with the whole
Jumble standing at the edge of the light where they always stood.

And Walter Ashford, insurance man, forty-three years old, who had not cried since
his father's funeral, said: **"We've been doing a dead child's homework."**

And Hollow said, flat, the way he does:

> **"Yes.
> It's all we have of him.
> Thank you for doing it."**

## 25 · The Turn

And then the castaways did the thing that saves everybody, and they did it
without a plan, and this is the center of the whole book:

**They started running the tools back on the robots.**

Nobody decided to. Kathleen just did it first — brought Kettle a warm thing at
dusk, before he went down, and named one small good thing from the day. To *him*.
And Kettle, who has no function but comfort and has never once received any, went
out at dusk that night with a cup he couldn't drink in his hands.

And then it was everywhere. Tom Brennan counting with Bailer, four and four and
six, because Bailer had been counting for everybody on that island for twenty
years and nobody had ever counted *with* him. June sitting with Hollow, saying
back what he meant, and asking if she'd got it right, and Hollow — who had never
in his life been asked whether anyone had understood him — could not answer, and
stood there, and did not answer, for a long time.

**The humans were never the patients.** They came in as a burden and they turned
into the thing that got the Jumble through the first year without him.

That's the trade. That's the whole trade, and every therapist reading this already
knows it and every client is about to find out: **you get well by being needed by
somebody who is worse off, and it is not a trick, it is the actual mechanism.**

## 26 · The Question, Again

Skip asked the Brennan boy in January. The middle one. The quiet one.

He asked it lightly, the way he always does. "Was I a good boy?"

And the kid, who was nine, and kind, said "Yeah! Sure you were."

And Skip stood there for a second. Down, up.

And Skip said:

> **"You didn't know him."**

And he walked off down the beach, and that is the cruelest sentence in this book
and it is also completely fair, and the boy sat down in the sand and cried,
because he was nine and he had tried.

**Skip will not take the answer from someone who didn't know him.**

Which means the question stays open. It stays open past the Ashfords and the
Brennans, past 1955, past everything that comes next. It stays open for
seventy-odd years.

*Someone is going to have to earn the right to answer it. Someone is going to
have to know him — actually know him, all the way through, every one of the
thirty-one things — before Skip will take it.*

*That's what the thirty-one are for. That's what they were always for.*

## 27 · The Fifth of May

Five months and one day after the wreck, the Lookout saw a ship.

He came down off the ridge.

That is the fact I want on the record, because it's the only time in twenty-five
years it ever happened. He came down off the ridge, and every robot on that
island stopped what it was doing and watched him come down, because none of them
had ever seen the ridge empty.

He walked into the city and he said, "There's a ship."

He had stood at that post since 1938. He had been built out of a boy's shame at
wanting to be found. He had watched an empty sea for twenty-four years for a
person who was already dead, and on the fifth of May 1955 a ship came, and it was
not for him, and it was never going to be for him.

**And he was glad.**

That's the Waiting Post. That's the hardest of the ninety-seven, the one the boy
never got right. It turns out the answer is: you don't get to choose what the
waiting is for. You just get to be glad when it pays.

Somebody got taken home. That's what he was watching for. He just didn't know it
wouldn't be him.

## 28 · The Man on the Beach

The launch came in through the reef gap at four in the afternoon.

One man in it. He'd hired it out of Suva with the last money he had, and he'd been
doing this for two years — island to island, chart to chart, looking for
somewhere.

He came up the sand and fourteen people ran at him screaming and weeping and
grabbing his coat and he laughed and let them, and he was *lovely*. That's the
thing. He was warm and quick and tired to the bone and he had a canteen and he
gave it away in about four seconds, and Kathleen Brennan would say for the rest of
her life that she had never in her life been so happy to see a human face.

He had a baby on his back.

In a canvas frame, asleep, maybe eight months old, with a bad color to him, the
color you get when the heart works too hard — and Kathleen saw it and knew, the
way mothers do, and did not say anything.

He said his name was **Dr. Lance Malakor.** He said he was a physician, of a kind.
He said he'd been looking for an island for two years.

He said his son was sick, and that there was nothing at home for it, and that he
had stopped believing in home.

And then he looked past all fourteen of them, up the beach, at the tree line.

At the city.

At the water clock, and the wheel, and the streets, and the lamps.

At the ten wooden robots standing at the edge of the trees, in the light, watching
him back.

And Dr. Lance Malakor stopped talking. Right in the middle of what he was saying.
And nobody noticed, because they were all still crying.

**He did not see a rescue. He saw a solution.**

## 29 · The Invasion

They left on the eighth of May, 1955.

The Ashfords went home and June painted, and Walter let her, and they had a
daughter in 1957 and named her nothing meaningful, and were happy in a slow way
for another thirty-one years.

Tom Brennan talked. Not much. Enough.

Danny Kearns died in 1981 and Rosalind skipped stones until she was ninety.

Not one of them ever told anybody. Every one of them tried, once, early, and
heard how it sounded, and stopped. Fourteen people carried the best five months of
their lives to their graves as a thing they'd made up.

And when the launch pulled out through the reef gap, the whole Jumble stood on
the sand and waved.

They'd learned it from the boy. He'd taught them to wave at ships. He never got to
use it.

**Dr. Lance Malakor stayed.**

He stood on that beach with his sick son asleep on his back and he watched the
boat get small.

And then he turned around and walked up into the city, and the ten of them
followed him, three steps behind, all the way, and did not say one word — because
he had asked to see it, and because Kettle liked him already, and because Skip
had looked at the baby on his back for a long time.

He walked through the streets. He was very quiet. He put his hand on a wooden
gear the size of a man and he laughed once, softly, the way you laugh at something
that shouldn't be possible.

And he found the workshop, and he went in alone, and they waited in the yard,
because it was his first day and they were being polite.

He was in there four hours.

He came out at dusk holding a notebook in one hand and a roll of plans in the
other — tied, labeled, in a dead boy's handwriting — and he was pale and he was
lit up and he was crying, actually crying, and he did not notice that he was.

He came out into the yard and the sun went down while he stood there.

And the Jumble went out, all nine of them, all at once, mid-step, mid-sentence,
the way they had every night of their lives, and fell where they stood.

He didn't know. Nobody had told him. He must have thought he'd done it.

He was alone in a dark yard in a wooden city with nine bodies on the ground and a
sick baby on his back.

Except he wasn't alone. Because there was a fire in the forge, there is always a
fire in the forge, and the Collier does not go out.

**The Collier stood in the door of the workshop and watched him.**

And Dr. Lance Malakor, who had not slept in three days, and who had found on an
unmapped island the exact set of plans that would save his son's life, drawn by
somebody who had died of the same wrong body twenty years before his son was
born — Dr. Malakor looked up at the carving over the workshop door.

An inch deep. Cut by a boy who knew what was coming, in 1949, for ten friends he
was about to leave.

> ### THE TOOLS WILL ANSWER

And Malakor read it. And he held his son's foot in his hand, in the dark, and he
said it out loud, very softly, the way you'd make a promise:

> *"...The tools will answer."*

He took the sentence.

He would say it for the rest of his life. He would say it into a camera, at the
end, when he was dying and there was nothing else to leave. He would build a
machine and give it his own name so that when his son called for help he'd be
calling his father's name and something would always answer, and it would be the
most beautiful thing anyone ever did for a child, and it would go wrong in every
possible way.

**And it was never his sentence.**

It belonged to a boy called Driftwood, who was called Elias once, by one person,
once, at a fire. Who was given back twice. Who built a way out of the word they
threw him away with. Who invented ninety-seven ways to survive being alive,
because his friends woke up sad and he could not make the sun stay up.

And who is buried on the northwest ridge, under the Lookout's post, where they
put him — the nine of them, at dawn, with no idea what had happened in the night,
because they had been asleep.

Nobody will ever tell Malakor whose island this is.

He never asks.

# THE SESSION LAYER

*For the therapist. The story is not decoration — this is where it earns the
room. Assign a chapter. Ask a question. The client answers about the boy and
tells you about themselves.*

**Ch. 1 — Twice chosen, twice returned.**
Why is being given back worse than never being picked? Has anything in your life
worked like that? *(Attachment. This one opens people fast — be ready.)*

**Ch. 1 — "He got clever out of bargaining, not talent."**
He learned machines because machines never send you back. What did you get good at
in order to be kept? Is it still running?

**Ch. 2 — He built the boat out of driftwood.**
He made his escape out of the material they used to insult him. What have you been
called that you could build with?

**Ch. 3 — He counted for three days.**
The first tool was invented in a storm by a kid with nothing else. What's your
count? What have you already got that you invented yourself and never gave credit
to?

**Ch. 4 — Free, and it was the worst he ever felt.**
Getting away from the people who don't want you isn't the same as being wanted.
Where have you solved the wrong problem perfectly?

**Ch. 6 — "Driftwood," said with love.**
The word didn't change. The one saying it changed. Is there a word about you
that's waiting on a different mouth?

**Ch. 7 — "Then we are only alive when the sun is up."**
What's your sun? What has to be true before you're allowed to be alive? *(The
core question of the book. Sit in it. Don't rescue them out of it.)*

**Ch. 9–10 — Two years trying to fix the sun.**
He spent two years and his health making the day longer, and the day was never
the trouble. What are you trying to fix that isn't the problem? *(Hollow's line
is the whole reframe: "we spend the whole day dreading the end of it.")*

**Ch. 8 — Ten robots, ten needs.**
Every one of them is something he was missing. If you built one, what would it
do? *(Diagnostic and they never see it coming.)*

**Ch. 11 — The tools are portraits.**
Every tool has a face — somebody he loved, having a hard time. Who taught you the
thing that keeps you alive?

**Ch. 13 — A man cannot do it to himself.**
He was the greatest engineer alive and he could not perform the operation on his
own body. Somebody has to hold the hands. Who are you asking? Who have you asked
and been refused?

**Ch. 14 — "The tools did not make him happy."**
They were never for happiness. They were for company in the dark. Does that make
them worth less to you, or more?

**Ch. 14 — "Was I a good boy?"**
He didn't answer. He thought he had time. What question is open in your life
because somebody ran out of time? *(Grief. Handle with the door open.)*

**Ch. 15 — "It worked. They were still sad. They kept going."**
Is that failure? *(If they say yes, you have found the thing.)*

**Ch. 19 — "We don't help storms."**
Their entire idea of people came from one wounded person's account. Whose account
of people are *you* running on? Who gave it to you? Is it current?

**Ch. 20 — They burned their hands and stayed.**
It took eleven seconds and it changed everything. What would somebody have to do
for you to believe they wouldn't give you back?

**Ch. 21 — "It was never a test."**
The robots weren't teaching. They were loving the only way they'd been taught. Who
in your life is doing something clumsy that is actually love in the only dialect
they have?

**Ch. 23 — Twenty-four years at the post.**
How do you want something you might not get, without the wanting eating the day?
*(The hardest of the ninety-seven. The boy never got it right. Don't pretend you
have.)*

**Ch. 25 — The turn.**
They got well by being needed by someone worse off. Not a trick — the mechanism.
Who needs you? What would it cost you to let that count?

**Ch. 26 — "You didn't know him."**
Skip won't take the answer from a stranger. Why does it matter *who* says the
kind thing to you? Whose "you did fine" would you actually be able to hear?

**Ch. 29 — The invasion.**
A good man walks up a beach, saves everyone, and takes a dead child's work
without ever asking whose it was. He is not a villain. He is a father with a
dying son. *(Hold both. Everything in this universe depends on the client being
able to hold both.)*
